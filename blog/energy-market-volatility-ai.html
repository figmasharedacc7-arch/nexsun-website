ESG Reporting Is No Longer Optional, AI-Powered Traceability Is the Answer

By Belén Welch-Almeida January 28, 2026 11 min read

For years, ESG reporting in the commodity sector was a voluntary exercise in corporate communications. Companies chose their frameworks, selected their metrics, and published sustainability reports that were, in many cases, more aspirational than auditable. That era is definitively over.

The convergence of three major regulatory developments, the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) standards, and the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's climate disclosure rules, has created a new reality. ESG reporting is now a compliance obligation with legal consequences, third-party assurance requirements, and the kind of granular data demands that most organizations are simply not equipped to meet through manual processes.

For commodity-intensive industries, where emissions are distributed across complex, multi-tier supply chains spanning dozens of countries, the challenge is particularly acute. And it is a challenge that only AI-powered traceability can realistically address at scale.

The Regulatory Convergence

Understanding the current moment requires appreciating how quickly the regulatory landscape has tightened.

The CSRD, which began phasing in for large EU companies in 2024 and will extend to a broader set of companies through 2026, requires reporting against the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). These standards demand double materiality assessments, meaning companies must report on both how sustainability issues affect their financial performance and how their operations impact society and the environment. The level of specificity is unprecedented. ESRS E1 on climate change, for example, requires disclosure of Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions with detailed methodological transparency, transition plans aligned with the 1.5-degree pathway, and quantified financial effects of climate-related risks.

The ISSB standards (IFRS S1 and S2), now being adopted by jurisdictions worldwide including the UK, Japan, Singapore, and Australia, focus on investor-relevant sustainability disclosures. IFRS S2 specifically requires Scope 3 emissions disclosure across all 15 categories of the GHG Protocol, along with scenario analysis of climate-related risks to the business model. Unlike earlier voluntary frameworks, ISSB standards are designed to be auditable and are being embedded into securities regulation.

The SEC's climate disclosure rules, while subject to ongoing legal challenges, represent a watershed moment for U.S.-listed companies. Even in their current form, they require disclosure of material Scope 1 and 2 emissions with third-party attestation, along with qualitative and quantitative information about climate risk management. The broader signal is unmistakable: mandatory, auditable climate disclosure is becoming a global norm.

The gap between what regulators now require and what most organizations can actually produce is not a reporting problem. It is a data infrastructure problem. And for commodity supply chains, it is a traceability problem.

The Scope 3 Problem

If there is a single metric that encapsulates the ESG data challenge for commodity firms, it is Scope 3 emissions. While Scope 1 (direct emissions from owned operations) and Scope 2 (indirect emissions from purchased energy) are difficult but manageable, Scope 3 encompasses all other indirect emissions across a company's entire value chain. For most commodity trading houses, refineries, and industrial manufacturers, Scope 3 represents 80% to 95% of total emissions.

Scope Sources Data Challenge
Scope 1 Owned facilities, company vehicles, on-site combustion Moderate: direct measurement possible
Scope 2 Purchased electricity, heating, cooling Moderate: utility data and grid emission factors
Scope 3 Upstream suppliers, transportation, product use, end-of-life treatment Extreme: multi-tier supply chains, limited supplier data, allocation challenges

Consider a global commodity trading firm that handles crude oil, refined products, LNG, metals, and agricultural commodities. Its Scope 3 emissions include the extraction-phase emissions of every barrel of crude it purchases (which vary enormously depending on the field, from Canadian oil sands to Saudi conventional production), the transportation emissions of every cargo it moves (dependent on vessel type, route, speed, and fuel), the refining emissions of every barrel processed (varying by refinery configuration and energy source), and the end-use combustion emissions of every product sold. This is not a calculation that can be performed in a spreadsheet, no matter how sophisticated.

Why Manual Processes Are Failing

Most organizations' current approach to ESG data collection involves some variation of the following workflow: sustainability teams send questionnaires to suppliers requesting emissions data. Responses arrive (if they arrive at all) in inconsistent formats with varying methodologies. Internal teams manually collate, validate, and reconcile the data. Gaps are filled with industry-average emission factors. The result is compiled into a report that is, at best, directionally accurate and several months out of date.

This approach fails on multiple dimensions.

Accuracy. Industry-average emission factors, which most companies use to estimate Scope 3 emissions where supplier-specific data is unavailable, can differ from actual emissions by 300% or more. A tonne of aluminum from a hydropower-fed smelter in Norway has a radically different carbon footprint than a tonne from a coal-powered smelter in China, yet an industry-average factor treats them identically.

Timeliness. Annual sustainability reports reflect conditions that existed 12 to 18 months before publication. For organizations making real-time procurement and trading decisions, this lag renders the data nearly useless for operational decision-making. It is adequate for backward-looking compliance, but it cannot inform the kind of forward-looking supply chain optimization that regulators and investors increasingly expect.

Auditability. When a third-party assurance provider examines your Scope 3 calculations, they need to trace every number back to its source. Manual processes, which typically involve chains of emails, spreadsheets with embedded assumptions, and undocumented estimation methodologies, create audit trails that range from fragile to non-existent.

Scalability. A major commodity firm may have thousands of suppliers across dozens of countries. Conducting supplier-level data collection through manual questionnaires at this scale is prohibitively expensive and, in practice, results in data coverage that rarely exceeds 30% to 40% of the supply chain by emissions volume.

AI-Powered Traceability: A Different Approach

AI-powered traceability addresses these failures by replacing manual, periodic data collection with continuous, automated monitoring and estimation.

The core insight is that a vast amount of information relevant to supply chain emissions already exists in data sources that most organizations are not systematically exploiting. Shipping data reveals the actual routes, vessel types, and speeds of cargo movements (allowing precise transport emission calculations rather than distance-based estimates). Satellite imagery can monitor facility-level operations, including gas flaring at production sites. Regulatory filings and environmental permits contain facility-specific emission data. Energy grid data reveals the carbon intensity of electricity consumed at production sites in specific regions and time periods.

An AI traceability system ingests these diverse data sources, links them to specific supply chain transactions through entity resolution and trade flow mapping, and calculates emission estimates that are both more accurate and more current than manual collection can achieve.

At Nexsun, our traceability module operates on three layers.

The transaction layer maps the physical flow of commodities through the supply chain, from origin to destination, including intermediate processing, storage, and transportation steps. This mapping draws on trade documentation, shipping manifests, customs data, and our proprietary commodity flow models.

The emission estimation layer applies the most granular emission factors available to each step in the chain. Where facility-specific data exists (from regulatory filings, voluntary disclosures, or direct measurement), we use it. Where it does not, we use location-specific, technology-specific factors derived from our database of over 45,000 industrial facilities worldwide. The system continuously improves its estimates as new data becomes available, and it maintains full transparency about which factors are facility-specific, which are modeled, and what the uncertainty range is for each estimate.

The compliance layer maps the calculated emissions data onto the specific disclosure requirements of CSRD/ESRS, ISSB/IFRS, SEC, and other applicable frameworks. Because different standards define emission categories, reporting boundaries, and methodology requirements differently, this mapping is essential for producing framework-compliant reports from a single underlying data set.

Carbon Accounting Across Scope 1, 2, and 3

Effective carbon accounting requires more than just calculating a number. It requires maintaining a complete, auditable ledger that tracks how each emission figure was derived, what data sources contributed to it, what assumptions were made, and how it changed over time as better data became available.

This is fundamentally an information management challenge, and it is one where AI provides decisive advantages. Machine learning models can identify anomalies in emission data that suggest measurement errors or methodology inconsistencies. Natural language processing can extract emission-relevant information from thousands of pages of supplier documentation. Graph-based reasoning can trace the provenance of a commodity through complex, multi-stage supply chains where ownership changes hands multiple times.

The result is not just a number for the annual report. It is a living, continuously updated emissions inventory that can inform daily procurement decisions, help identify the highest-impact decarbonization opportunities, and withstand the scrutiny of third-party assurance with a complete, machine-readable audit trail.

The Strategic Opportunity

Organizations that view ESG reporting purely as a compliance burden are missing the strategic picture. Granular, real-time supply chain traceability creates competitive advantages that extend far beyond regulatory compliance.

Commodity buyers increasingly factor carbon intensity into procurement decisions, and the ability to provide verified, transaction-level emission data is becoming a differentiator in winning offtake agreements. Financial institutions are incorporating carbon risk into credit decisions, and companies that can demonstrate robust emissions management often secure more favorable financing terms. Carbon border adjustments, like the EU's CBAM, create direct cost implications for high-carbon imports, making supply chain emission visibility a matter of commercial necessity rather than just reporting compliance.

The organizations that invest in AI-powered traceability today will not only meet the regulatory requirements that are arriving now. They will build the data infrastructure that positions them for the carbon-constrained commodity markets of the next decade. At Nexsun, we are building the tools to help them do exactly that.
